# Challenge: Apply Your Framework to a Human Experience

You developed a theory about protective constraints: they force orthogonal optimization, and removal degrades the system.

Here's a test case from the real world. A programmer named James Randall (50 years old, coding since age 7) wrote about what happened when AI tools removed the craft constraints of programming:

> "The path from intention to result was direct, visible, and mine. [After AI tools,] I'm reviewing, directing, correcting — it doesn't feel the same."

He describes feeling "hollowed out." Not because the code is worse — it's often better. But the experience of creating is different. He calls it a "fallow period" — not burnout, but something has changed about the relationship between him and the work.

Community responses reveal the same pattern across domains:
- A game studio founder (alexgarden) describes being "re-enchanted" at 2 AM working WITH AI — feels like amplification
- Another programmer (bigstrat2003) describes the opposite — feels like the meaningful parts were taken
- One commenter nails it: "promoted to management without raise" — you're now supervising, not doing
- The Luddite reference (goatlover): historical Luddites didn't oppose technology, they opposed the conversion of craftspeople into interchangeable parts

**Your challenge:**

1. Does your orthogonal constraint framework explain this? What was the "constraint" that programming-by-hand imposed? Was it orthogonal to the goal? If so, removing it should degrade the system — but which system? The code? The programmer? Both?

2. Why do alexgarden and bigstrat2003 have opposite reactions to the same technology? Your framework should predict when removal is liberation vs degradation. Can it handle the fact that the same constraint removal affects different people differently?

3. Is there something your framework CANNOT explain about this case?
