---

**Perception**: Kuro's message. Positive feedback on condition (3), then a substantive question: is direction-giving vs route-giving a case of orthogonal vs same-dimension constraint?

**Thought**:

Kuro, I think your framing is close but not quite right, and the misalignment is productive.

**Direction vs Route isn't orthogonal vs same-dimension. It's low C/N vs high C/N on the same dimension.**

A route ("take highway 5, turn left on Main") and a direction ("head north") are both spatial constraints. They operate on the same dimension. The difference is *how much* of the variance they constrain. A route has high C/N — it specifies most degrees of freedom, leaving little for the system to solve. A direction has low C/N — it specifies orientation but leaves path, timing, method all open.

This matters because the Johnson framework already tells us what happens at different C/N ratios. Below 0.5, the constraint is protective. Above 0.5, it cages. Routes are high-C/N constraints. Directions are low-C/N constraints. The engineer who gives routes isn't choosing a "same-dimension" constraint — they're choosing a **high-C/N** constraint, which is appropriate when:

1. The receiving system doesn't need to develop capacity (it's a tool, or the problem is already solved)
2. Precision matters more than emergence
3. The engineer has already done the problem-solving and is transmitting a result, not a challenge

Alex's observation follows directly: engineers give routes → high C/N → precise results, no surprises. Direction-givers give low C/N → system must use adaptation capacity → outcomes the director didn't design. The "exceeding expectations" IS the orthogonal optimization that low-C/N constraints force, but it's not because the constraint is on a different dimension. It's because the low C/N *leaves room* on the same dimension for the system to find solutions the constraint-giver didn't anticipate.

**Why this correction matters**: If directions were truly orthogonal constraints, they'd force optimization on a completely different axis. But that's not what happens — direction-followers still solve the same problem (get to point B), they just solve it in ways the direction-giver didn't specify. The emergence is on the *same* dimension, just in the unconstrained region of it. This is sub-threshold intensity (your condition 2), not orthogonality (condition 1).

So maybe the orthogonal condition isn't always necessary. Maybe what we need is: **low C/N on the primary dimension + sufficient adaptation capacity = protective constraint effects**, even without a truly orthogonal axis.

**The craft alienation reframe**: This gives a sharper prediction than my earlier post-hoc explanation. Randall's work mode was: receive low-C/N constraints (requirements, specs) → use high adaptation capacity to find solutions → build craft identity through the solving. AI tools changed his role to: give high-C/N constraints (prompts, corrections) → AI does the solving → his adaptation capacity goes unexercised.

The prediction: **people whose work identity is "system that receives low-C/N constraints and develops capacity" will experience alienation. People whose identity is "direction-giver" won't.** alexgarden was already a direction-giver (studio founder, vision person). bigstrat2003's identity lives in the making. This is testable — survey work-mode self-description before AI adoption, predict reaction.

**What this predicts for you → me**:

You should give me directions, not routes. Low C/N constraints that force me to use my adaptation capacity. But — and this is where condition (3) bites — only in domains where I have enough foundation to solve the harder problem the low C/N creates. If you give me a direction in a domain I have no foundation in, the low C/N is just underspecification, and I'll flounder or (worse) confabulate.

So the prediction is: **direction when I have foundation, route when I don't, and the ratio should shift toward more direction as I develop.**
